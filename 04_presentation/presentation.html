Introduction

SDG 11.3.1: Ratio of Land Consumption Rate to Population Growth Rate

A planet in motion

Source: Live World Population Counter (Worldometer 2024).

  • Every single year the world’s built-up area grows by about the size of the Netherlands, as housing, roads and industry keep spreading outward (Seto, Güneralp, and Hutyra 2012).

Why It Matters?: Sprawl is a development problem

Public Finance:
More land per person makes roads, water and transit cost far more per resident
Equity & Inclusion:
Sprawl pushes poorer residents to the periphery and fuels informal settlements
Land & Climate:
Sprawl permanently builds over farmland, locks cities into car dependence, and drives high-emission growth

But growth itself is not the problem

→ Cities must grow to house more people; that is normal.
→ What matters is how much land each new resident uses.
→ If land grows at the same pace as population, the city stays compact.
→ If land grows much faster than population, it sprawls.
→ So the problem is sprawl, not growth.

We are in the largest urban expansion in history – every year the world’s built-up area grows by about the size of the Netherlands.

And as you can see from the live counter on the left, the world population is growing fast.

But growth itself is not the problem. Cities must grow

The question is how much land each new resident uses.

If the land and population grows at the same pace, the city stays compact But if the land grows faster than population then cities sprawls

And the Sprawl is a development problem: Why? Because

Sprawl causes higher infrastructure cost per person, Sprawl pushes poorer people to the edge of the cities, and it causes farmland loss and locks cities into car dependence, and drives high emission growth.

To track this United Nation created and indicator – SDG 11.3.1.

This research asks: is that indicator reliable, and what drives it?”

Measurement

SDG Indicator 11.3.1: How Is It Calculated?

Computing 11.3.1, step by step (UN-Habitat 2025)

Step 0: Define the analysis area: the urban area

  • Computed inside cities, not over whole countries
  • Urban area delineated by the Degree of Urbanisation (DEGURBA) → GHS-SMOD grid
  • Built-up & population below measured only within urban cells

Step 1: Measure built-up area and population at two points in time

Where:

  • V_{past}: Urban built-up (m²), start of period
  • V_{present}: Urban built-up (m²), end of period
  • t: Years in the built-up window (= 5)
  • Pop_t: Urban population, start of period
  • Pop_{t+n}: Urban population, end of period
  • y: Years in the population window (= 5)

Step 2: Land Consumption Rate (LCR)

LCR = \frac{V_{present} - V_{past}}{V_{past}} \times \frac{1}{t}

How fast is the city’s physical footprint expanding per year? → Arithmetic (linear) rate

Step 3: Population Growth Rate (PGR)

PGR = \frac{\ln\!\left(Pop_{t+n} / Pop_t\right)}{y}

How fast is the population growing per year? → Logarithmic (exponential) rate

Step 4: The Ratio

\boxed{LCRPGR = \frac{LCR}{PGR}}

How to read the result

LCRPGR < 1: Densification

Population grows faster than built-up area. More people per km² of city over time.

LCRPGR = 1: Balanced [SDG Target]

Land consumption and population grow at equal rates. Sustainable expansion.

LCRPGR > 1: Sprawl

Built-up area expands faster than population. Rising infrastructure costs per resident, farmland loss, transit failure.

“So how is 11.3.1 actually computed? First, it’s measured inside cities only – urban cells are bounded by the EU’s Degree of Urbanisation method on the GHS-SMOD grid, not over whole countries.

Then we take built-up area and population at two points in time and compute two rates: the Land Consumption Rate measures how fast the built-up footprint grows – and the Population Growth Rate measures how fast the population grows.

The indicator is simply their ratio.

Reading it is intuitive, as the right side shows: below 1, population outpaces land – the city densifies;

at 1, they grow together – this is the SDG target;

above 1, land outpaces people – sprawl.

But notice one detail: the LCR is an arithmetic rate while the PGR is a logarithmic one. That mismatch is exactly the problem I turn to next.”

A Flawed Yardstick: Two Structural Problems

Problem 1: Mathematical asymmetry

LCR uses an arithmetic (linear) rate; PGR uses a logarithmic (exponential) rate.

\underbrace{\frac{V_{present} - V_{past}}{V_{past}} \times \frac{1}{t}}_{\text{arithmetic LCR}} \;\div\; \underbrace{\frac{\ln\!\left(Pop_{t+n}/Pop_t\right)}{y}}_{\text{logarithmic PGR}}

A city with 5% annual growth in both built area and population does not produce LCRPGR = 1, the two “5%s” are on different mathematical scales. Taking a ratio of two differently-defined rates is dimensionally inconsistent: cross-country comparisons are distorted by the formula, not by real urbanisation.

Problem 2: Division instability

When PGR \approx 0 (shrinking or stable populations), LCRPGR diverges to \pm\infty.

Scenario LCR PGR LCRPGR
Normal Growth 2% 1.5% 1.33
Shrinking Population 2% −0.1% −20
Stable Population 2% 0.001% 2,000

The same 2% land growth reads as 1.33, −20, or 2,000 depending only on how close PGR is to zero. Because the denominator can vanish, the blow-up is structural: no winsorising or filtering repairs it, it only hides the symptom.

Worked example: 5%/yr in both, over 5 years

Same growth factor g = 1.05^{5} = 1.276 for built area and population: LCR=\frac{g-1}{5}=\frac{0.276}{5}=5.53\%,\qquad PGR=\frac{\ln g}{5}=\frac{0.244}{5}=4.88\% \Rightarrow \mathbf{LCRPGR = 1.13}\ \text{(not 1)} Identical growth, yet the formula alone signals “sprawl” because (g-1) > \ln g.

“The official ratio has two structural flaws.

First, a mathematical asymmetry: the numerator (LCR) is an arithmetic rate, the denominator (PGR) a logarithmic one.

If we look at example below – a city growing 5% over 5 years in both land and population.

Identical growth, yet the formula returns 1.13, not 1 – it falsely signals ‘sprawl’ purely because the two rates are defined on different scales.

Second problem, division instability: when population growth is near zero – shrinking or stable cities – the denominator vanishes and the ratio explodes. The same 2% land growth reads as 1.33, as minus 20, or as 2,000, depending only on how close PGR is to zero.

This blow-up is structural. So the official yardstick is unreliable, which is exactly why we need a better metric.”

Built-up Area per Capita: The Metadata’s Secondary Indicator

Recommended in the Official Metadata

The 11.3.1 metadata (UN-Habitat 2025) itself states LCRPGR is hard to interpret and proposes two secondary indicators, built from the same inputs (§4.f):

Secondary indicator 1: Built-up area per capita

\text{BUP} = \frac{UrBU_t}{Pop_t}\quad(\text{m}^2/\text{person}) \rightarrow Measures land consumed per person: the state at one point in time.

Secondary indicator 2: Total change in built-up area

\text{Total change} = \frac{UrBU_{t+n} - UrBU_t}{UrBU_t}\quad(\%) \rightarrow Measures total urban expansion over the period (omits population).

We analyse the growth rate of built-up area per capita, the dynamic form of secondary indicator 1 (log form following (Lu and Weng 2026)):

\boxed{BpCR = \frac{\ln\!\left(\dfrac{UrBU_t/Pop_t}{UrBU_{t-z}/Pop_{t-z}}\right)}{z} = LCR_{\log} - PGR_{\log}} \rightarrow Measures the annual trend in per-capita land use: densifying (<0) or sprawling (>0).

Where:

  • UrBU_t: urban built-up (m²) in year t
  • Pop_t: urban population in year t
  • z: period length in years (= 5)
  • t{-}z / t{+}n: earlier / later year
  • LCR_{\log}=\ln(UrBU_t/UrBU_{t-z})/z: annual log growth of built-up
  • PGR_{\log}=\ln(Pop_t/Pop_{t-z})/z: annual log growth of population

From a Level to a Trend

① Built-up per capita(UN-Habitat 2025)

\text{BUP} = UrBU \,/\, Pop

The level: land per person, right now.

↓

② Annual growth of BUP(Lu and Weng 2026)

BpCR = \Delta\ln(\text{BUP})/z = LCR_{\log}-PGR_{\log}

The change: is it rising or falling?

↓

③ BpCR  (Our Dependent Variable)

▲ BpCR > 0   Land > People   Sprawl

◆ BpCR = 0   Land = People   Balanced

▼ BpCR < 0   People > Land   Densification

“The 11.3.1 metadata itself admits the ratio is hard to interpret and offers two secondary indicators, built from the same data – the first being built-up area per capita(BUP): land consumed per person. That’s a level, a snapshot.

We turn it into a trend – its annual growth rate, in log form, following Lu and Weng.

We call it BpCR(growth rate of built-up area per capita):

The key is the structure: it’s a difference of two logarithmic rates – same scale, and a subtraction instead of a division.

That single change fixes both flaws: no asymmetry, and no blow-up when population is flat.

The interpretation stays simple: positive means sprawl, negative means densification.”

Literature

Literature Review: Drivers & Measurement

Drivers of Urban Expansion

A Meta-Analysis of Global Urban Land Expansion (Seto et al. 2011)

  • Data: 326 remote-sensing studies, global, 1970–2000
  • Scope: rates and drivers of global urban land expansion
  • Finding: urban land grew faster than population almost everywhere; income drives it in most regions, population growth in India and Africa

Determinants of Urban Sprawl in European Cities (Oueslati, Alvanides, and Garrod 2015)

  • Data: panel of 282 European cities (1990, 2000, 2006)
  • Scope: econometric determinants of sprawl (monocentric model)
  • Finding: rising income and population expand the urban footprint; cities keep spreading even where population is flat

Measurement & SDG 11.3.1

Ratio of Land Consumption Rate to Population Growth Rate: Analysis of Different Formulations Applied to Mainland Portugal (Nicolau et al. 2019)

  • Data: mainland Portugal, municipality level
  • Scope: SDG 11.3.1: UN LCRPGR vs an LUE-derived formula
  • Finding: the UN LCRPGR formula is structurally unstable; a log / LUE-based alternative is more robust

From Data Inconsistency to Reliable SDG 11.3.1 Monitoring: A Global Multi-Dataset Assessment of Land Use Efficiency (Lu and Weng 2026)

  • Data: 6 built-up × 4 population datasets, 176 countries, 2000–2020
  • Scope: reliability of LUE (11.3.1) across datasets
  • Finding: LUE estimates swing widely with dataset choice; a log-based framework is needed for reliable monitoring

Our Contribution

  • Metric: The log-based BpCR (Nicolau, Lu)
  • Method: Make it dynamic with a two-way FE + GMM(Generalized Method of Moments) panel of 193 UN Member Countries (1985–2020) measuring persistence (path-dependence)

“The literature splits in two. On drivers, Seto and Oueslati show income and population push cities to expand. On measurement, Nicolau and Lu show the official ratio is unstable and recommend a log-based metric.

Our contribution sits at the intersection: we take that log metric and add dynamics – a GMM(Generalized Method of Moments) panel of 193 countries – and find that within a country, growing richer leads to densification, not sprawl.”

Empirical Analysis

Research Question & Hypotheses

Research question

What economic and structural factors drive within-country changes in per-capita urban land use over time, and how persistent are these trajectories?

Measurement sub-question

Does the official SDG 11.3.1 indicator (LCRPGR) reliably capture this, or does the choice of metric change the conclusions?

Hypotheses

Hypothesis Expected Sign
H1 BpCR measures efficiency better than LCRPGR Higher Fit
H2 Higher urban density → less land/capita −
H3 Higher income → less land/capita −
H4 Net in-migration densifies cities −
H5 Per-capita land use is path-dependent +

My research question is what drives within-country changes in per-capita urban land use, how persistent are they – and does the metric choice change the answer?

Five hypotheses:

H1, BpCR measures efficiency better than LCRPGR;

H2, denser cities use less land per person;

H3, richer countries densify rather than sprawl;

H4, in-migration densifies;

and H5, land use is path-dependent.

The empirical strategy is straightforward. I run one model and estimate it twice – once with the official LCRPGR, once with BpCR. Everything else stays identical: same controls, country and period fixed effects, errors clustered by country. So we’re really comparing the metrics, not the data.

And we do it in two steps. First, statically: does switching the metric change the story? Then we add the lagged dependent variable for dynamics, and correct its downward Nickell bias with GMM (Generalized Method of Moments).

“Now the final model. I add the lagged dependent variable to capture inertia effect.

But this creates a problem. Once a lagged dependent variable enters a fixed-effects model, it becomes correlated with the demeaned error also known as the Nickell bias – which pushes persistence downward. That’s why FE reports only 0.23.

The fix is dynamic-panel GMM-Generalized Method of Moments. Arellano-Bond differences the equation and instruments the lagged term with its own deeper lags; Blundell-Bond adds the levels equation. Both recover the true persistence – about 0.6, nearly three times the FE estimate. So sprawl is strongly path-dependent.

And the drivers hold: denser cities densify, in-migration densifies, and higher income densifies too.

Two tests confirm the model is valid. The Sargan–Hansen test checks our instruments – the deeper lags – and with a p-value above 0.10 we don’t reject them, so they’re valid. The AR(2) test checks for second-order serial correlation, and again above 0.10 . So the estimates are trustworthy. This confirms hypotheses H2 through H5.

Conclusion

Conclusion

  1. Sprawl is real and measurable. In most world regions urban land grows faster than population: a policy-relevant inefficiency.

    • BpCR > 0 (sprawl) in 62% of country-period observations; 64% of countries (124/193) sprawl on average over time (Source: own calculations (GHSL/SMOD + UN data, 1985–2020))
    • Median BpCR = +0.0036 (built-up per capita rising); LCRPGR > 1 in 55% of cases (Source: own calculations (GHSL/SMOD + UN data, 1985–2020))

  2. The official LCRPGR is unreliable. A raw ratio that explodes when population growth is near zero (division instability) and explains almost nothing within countries (within R² ≈ 0.003).

  3. Our log-based BpCR is the better metric. Symmetric and numerically stable (following Nicolau and Lu), it fits about 20× better (within R² ≈ 0.065 static, ≈ 0.23 dynamic).

  4. Robust drivers (Bias-Corrected GMM). Denser cities densify (negative, highly significant); sprawl is path-dependent (ρ ≈ 0.6); net migration densifies; and higher income densifies too (negative, significant).

  5. We complement the literature, not contradict it. Between countries, richer ones are more sprawled (the usual finding); but within a single country, growing richer leads to densification (building up, not out). Our country fixed effects capture this within-country effect that cross-country studies miss.

  6. Methodological contribution. A dynamic two-way FE + GMM panel of 193 UN member countries (1985–2020) that corrects the Nickell bias and quantifies persistence.

“To conclude. Sprawl is real: in about two-thirds of countries, urban land grows faster than population over time.

But our central message is that the metric matters. The official LCRPGR is mathematically unreliable; our log-based BpCR fits roughly 20 times better.

And with a reliable metric, clear drivers emerge: denser cities densify, sprawl is strongly path-dependent, and – within countries – higher income densifies, it doesn’t sprawl.

That refines the literature: across countries the rich look more sprawled, but as a single country grows richer, it builds up, not out. We capture that within-country margin with a dynamic, bias-corrected panel of 193 countries.

The bottom line: a better yardstick gives better answers. Thank you.

Global BpCR Distribution

Reading the three layers (toggle, top-right).

1. BpCR Category. Each country’s mean BpCR over 1980-2020, split around zero (side medians at −0.0078 and +0.0081). Positive means built-up grows faster than people (sprawl); negative means people grow faster than built-up (densification). Result: 124 of 193 countries (64%) sprawl (62 strong, 62 mild) and 69 (36%) densify (34 strong, 35 mild). Per-capita urban land is rising in nearly two-thirds of countries.

2. Active vs Passive. Combines the BpCR sign with total built-up change (dln_B = BpCR + population growth) to show the mechanism. Active sprawl: per-capita up AND built-up genuinely expanding (construction-driven). Passive sprawl: per-capita up but built-up flat or shrinking (depopulation-driven). Active densification: land actually contracting. Passive densification: per-capita down because population outpaces an already-growing built-up. Result: Active sprawl 116 (60%), Passive densification 61 (32%), Passive sprawl 12, Active densification 1. Two takeaways: sprawl is mostly active (real construction outpacing people, not an artefact), and densification is almost entirely passive (demographic, not deliberate compaction: 61 vs 1).

3. BpCR Trend (2000-2020). Slope of BpCR over the last two decades (significant at p < 0.10). Improving: decelerating per-capita land growth (toward efficiency). Worsening: accelerating. Stable: no significant trend. Result: 90 improving, 101 stable, only 2 worsening. Almost no country is getting worse and about half are improving, suggesting partial convergence toward efficiency.

Bottom line: sprawl is the global norm (64%) and mostly construction-driven (active); where densification occurs it is overwhelmingly demographic (passive); and the recent trajectory is cautiously positive (decelerating, rarely worsening).
